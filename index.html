I only offer this service for tracts less than 3 acres. My topographic surveys map the elevations and features of your land. Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to common questions about land surveying

How long does a boundary survey typically take?

The timeline for a boundary survey varies depending on several factors, including property size, location, accessibility, and the complexity of the legal description. Generally, you can expect the field work to take 1-2 days, with an additional 1-2 weeks for research, calculations, and drafting the final survey map.

What does a boundary survey entail?

A boundary survey involves researching deeds and recorded plats, measuring the land using professional equipment, and locating or setting property corners. I'll then prepare a map that shows the boundary lines, any monuments found or set, and other relevant features. If you're requesting stakes or flags in the field, I can do that too - just let me know up front.

What's the difference between a boundary survey and property line staking?

A boundary survey is a comprehensive process that includes research of property records, field measurements, and the creation of a detailed map showing the legal boundaries of your property. Property line staking is an additional service where I place wooden stakes along your property lines to provide a visual reference. Line staking is typically added to a boundary survey, not performed as a standalone service.

Can I get just one line marked?

In most cases, I need to verify the entire boundary before staking even a single line. Property lines relate to each other, and errors in one part can affect another. Even if you're only concerned with one side, I still need to:

  • Research and analyze the full deed description
  • Locate or confirm corners on all sides
  • Cross-check measurements to make sure everything fits

I can prioritize the area you're most concerned about (like a fence or driveway), but I still need to survey the full boundary to do it accurately. That's why pricing reflects a complete boundary survey - not just one line.

Do I need to be present during the survey?

You don't need to be present during the entire survey process, but it can be helpful to meet with me at some point during the field work. This gives you an opportunity to ask questions and point out any specific concerns you might have. I'll coordinate with you in advance to arrange a convenient time.

How much does a land survey cost?

Survey costs vary widely depending on property size, location, terrain, vegetation, the complexity of the deed research, and the type of survey needed. Rather than providing a general estimate, I offer free quotes tailored to your property and needs.

It's been a few weeks - why haven't I heard from you?

I'm a one-person business, and I handle everything from field work to office work. If I haven't reached out, it's likely I'm still catching up from earlier projects. I appreciate your patience and promise that I haven't forgotten about you.

Do I need to clear the land before a survey?

Not necessarily. I can usually work around vegetation, but if the brush is very thick, it may slow things down or make it harder to mark the lines clearly. If you want the lines staked and easy to see, clearing helps.

Do you mark utilities?

No, but if utilities are marked before I arrive, I can locate visible markings during the survey. For public utilities, call 811 or visit Tennessee811. For private utilities, you'll need to hire a private locator. Unmarked utilities won't be picked up or shown.

What information do you need to give me a quote?

To provide an accurate quote, we typically need the property address, county, approximate lot size, the type of survey you need, and a brief description of why you need it (e.g., fence installation, property purchase). The contact form includes all these fields.

Do you offer subdividing services?

At this time, Chesnut Land Surveying specializes exclusively in existing residential boundary retracements for existing lots.


I do not offer subdivision services (dividing a larger tract into new, smaller parcels). Subdividing land in Tennessee is usually a complex administrative process that involves formal Planning Commission reviews, specific county platting regulations, and often requires coordinated efforts with Civil Engineers for drainage and utility design.


As a solo practitioner, I focus my resources on providing high-accuracy boundary work for individual homeowners. For new land divisions or commercial developments, I recommend contacting a larger, multi-disciplinary firm that has the dedicated administrative staff to navigate the county planning and approval process.
